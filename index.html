When two p implies q statements are given

When faced with two “If P, then Q” statements in a reasoning task, the key is to understand how these statements interact with each other to draw accurate conclusions. Here’s a structured approach to analyze and integrate the information provided by such conditional statements:

  • Identify the Statements:
    Begin by clearly identifying the antecedents (P) and the consequents (Q) in both statements.
    For example: if the first statement is “If it rains (P1), then the ground will be wet (Q1),” and the second statement is “If the ground is wet (P2), then the grass will grow (Q2),” recognize how P1 leads to Q1, which then becomes P2 leading to Q2.
  • Analyze Logical Flow:
    Look at how the consequent of the first statement relates to the antecedent of the second. This sequential link can often allow you to construct a new, derived conditional statement through a logical process known as syllogism.
    Example: since Q1 is the same as P2, you can deduce a new statement: “If it rains, then the grass will grow.”
  • Determine Validity of Options:
    With the combined understanding, evaluate the truth of each provided option by checking for consistency with your derived conclusion. Any option that contradicts the logical flow established by the linked statements is incorrect. Conversely, options that align with or are supported by your derived statement are likely to be correct.
  • Consider Contrapositives and Other Logical Forms:
    Remember that the contrapositive of a true conditional statement is also true.
    Example: the contrapositive of “If it rains, then the grass will grow” is “If the grass does not grow, then it does not rain.” This can be a powerful tool for eliminating options that may seem plausible but fail to hold logically.
